Stage 1 · Free-flow discovery

For every segment, slide a 30-min window across weekdays of 2-min data, pick the 3 quietest windows, pool their raw travel times, take p15.
Why we don't trust static speed limits or vendor "freeflow" — Urban signs say 50 km/h but real geometry + signal density often caps you at 35. Vendor freeflow columns are heuristic blends of nighttime averages and road-class defaults; the DB's freeflow_travel_time_sec mirrors the 8–9 am commute median within ±1 s on every Pune segment, which means using it would silently anchor regime classification to a congested baseline. Discovering free flow from each segment's own quietest 30 min is the only defensible baseline.

Stage 2 · Regime classification

For every 2-min bucket compute speed_ratio = ff_tt / current_tt. Bucket into 4 regimes grounded in the fundamental diagram of traffic flow.
Why 0.80 / 0.50 / 0.30 and not other numbers — These breakpoints come from the Greenshields / Van Aerde family of speed–density models. A road at 80% of free-flow speed is physically near capacity (stable dense flow). At 50% you're past the critical speed (LOS E). At 30% you're in stop-and-go (LOS F). They are traffic-engineering numbers, not tuned knobs.

Stage 3 · Bertini active-bottleneck test (core)

The three-point test: look at each segment with its upstream and downstream neighbour. A real bottleneck has a queue upstream AND free discharge downstream.
Why three points and not just one — A slow segment and a bottleneck look identical on a speed-only map (both are red). The difference is what's happening on either side. A bottleneck makes a queue back up upstream because demand outstrips capacity at the choke — and past the choke, traffic discharges freely because you're downstream of the restriction. Bertini & Leal's 2005 insight: use the three-point pattern as the operational definition of active bottleneck. Physically verifiable, no thresholds to tune.

Stage 3 R3 · Head-segment bottleneck (v2.1 addition)

The classical Bertini test needs an upstream segment, so S01 can never fire. R3 is a two-point relaxation for S01 specifically.
Why R3 matters — On a recorded corridor, the "first" segment is almost always the place where a junction queue starts. There's no S00 to act as the upstream anchor, so classical Bertini has a structural blind spot at S01. R3 adds a two-point relaxation: if S01 is CONG/SEVR and S02 is FREE/APPR, that IS a head bottleneck.

Stage 4 · Shockwave cross-check (LWR)

Mode: per-day onsets. For each adjacent pair, check whether the observed congestion onset lag matches the LWR prediction (backward propagation at 12–22 km/h).
Why shockwave validation is a check, not a gate — Bertini is already strong on its own. Shockwaves have a physical meaning: when a bottleneck forms, the congestion boundary propagates backwards at 12–22 km/h (LWR model). Low pass rate doesn't mean Bertini is wrong — it usually means the congestion is local (doesn't propagate far), or the onset signal is noisy.

Stage 5 · Systemic vs Point classification

Two independent rules: v2's simultaneity (≥80%) and v2.1's contiguity (≥60% of length). Either triggers SYSTEMIC.
Why two rules and not one — v2's simultaneity rule (≥80% of segments CONG at the same bucket) catches the canonical corridor-wide jam. v2.1 adds a contiguity rule (≥60% of corridor length in one unbroken CONG run) to catch corridors where 7 of 10 adjacent segments jam in a connected spine — operationally systemic even if the full 80% never fires simultaneously.

Stage 6 · Recurrence typing

How often does each segment show a congestion onset on an analysed weekday?
Why recurrence typing matters — A verdict of ACTIVE_BOTTLENECK only says "the three-point test fires"; it says nothing about how often. Operators prioritise by recurrence: a RECURRING bottleneck (≥75% of weekdays) is a standing issue; an OCCASIONAL one may be event-driven and not worth retuning for. Why this doesn't change verdicts — If a segment is perpetually saturated, its "free flow" is really "less bad congestion", which would make every regime classification wrong.
